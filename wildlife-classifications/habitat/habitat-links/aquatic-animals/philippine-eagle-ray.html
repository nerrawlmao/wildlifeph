Philippine Eagle Ray

Aetobatus narutobiei

The Philippine eagle ray (Aetobatus narutobiei) is a species of eagle ray found in the coastal waters of the Philippines. It is known for its distinctive dark coloration and large size, with a wingspan that can reach up to 3 meters. This majestic ray is a powerful swimmer and feeds mainly on mollusks and crustaceans on the sea floor.

Appearance

The Philippine eagle ray has a broad, flat body with a distinctive dark brown to black dorsal surface and a white underside. It possesses a long, whip-like tail with venomous spines near its base used for defense. Its head is characterized by a pronounced snout resembling an eagle's beak, giving the species its name.

The Philippine eagle ray inhabits shallow coastal waters, often near coral reefs and sandy bottoms. It is a benthic feeder that searches for mollusks and crustaceans buried in the substrate. This species is also known to migrate seasonally along the coastlines.

Philippine eagle rays are ovoviviparous, giving birth to live young after internal egg development. Mothers nurture their pups until they are ready to survive independently.

Conservation Status

Population TrendDecreasing

The Philippine eagle ray faces threats from habitat degradation, fishing bycatch, and coastal development. Conservation efforts focus on protecting critical habitats and reducing bycatch.

Population Threats

Major threats include fishing pressure, habitat loss from coastal development, pollution, and accidental capture in fishing gear.

Population Number

The exact population size is unknown due to limited studies, but populations are suspected to be declining in several areas.

Ecological Niche

As a benthic predator, the Philippine eagle ray helps control populations of benthic invertebrates, contributing to the balance of coastal marine ecosystems.
