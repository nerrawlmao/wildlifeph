Philippine Eagle

Pithecophaga jefferyi

The Philippine eagle is considered the largest of the extant eagles in the world in terms of length and wing surface area, with only Steller's sea eagle and the Harpy eagle being larger in terms of weight and bulk. It has been declared the national bird of the Philippines. It is also depicted in the Philippine one thousand-peso note.

Appearance

The Philippine eagle is considered the largest of all extant eagles in terms of length and wing surface, with only the Steller's sea eagle and the Harpy eagle being larger in average mass and bulk. It is the national bird of the Philippines. It has a dark face and a creamy-brown nape and crown.

Habitat & Ecology

    Biome Terrestrial, Forest Habitat Type Tropical, Subtropical/Tropical moist lowland forest Climate Zone Tropical

The Philippine eagle is endemic to the Philippines and found on four major islands: Luzon, Mindanao, Samar, and Leyte. The largest populations reside on Mindanao, where the species occupies the rugged, forested slopes of the island's mountain ranges. It requires large forest tracts to successfully raise a chick, since the species is highly territorial.

Population Threats

The Philippine eagle is critically endangered, primarily due to habitat loss and fragmentation resulting from deforestation. This deforestation is driven by logging, mining, agricultural expansion, and urbanization. Additionally, hunting and trapping of eagles, both for food and trade, pose a significant threat. Eagles are also believed to possess medicinal properties, further incentivizing their capture. Human disturbance near nesting sites can also lead to nest abandonment and failed breeding attempts. Climate change further exacerbates these threats through increased frequency of strong typhoons and altered forest ecosystems.

Population Number

The current population is estimated at 100-500 individuals in the wild (BirdLife International, 2020). This species is classified as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List, and its numbers are still decreasing.

Ecological Niche

The Philippine eagle occupies the ecological niche of an apex predator in its forest habitat. It plays a crucial role in regulating populations of smaller mammals, birds, and reptiles, thereby maintaining the balance of the ecosystem. As a top carnivore, its presence indicates a healthy and intact forest environment. The decline of the Philippine eagle can have cascading effects on the ecosystem, potentially leading to imbalances in prey populations and overall biodiversity.
