Philippine Tarsier

Carlito syrichta

The Philippine Tarsier is a tiny, nocturnal primate endemic to the southeastern Philippines, recognized for its enormous eyes and remarkable leaping abilities.

Common Name

Philippine Tarsier

Scientific Name

Carlito syrichta

Body Length

85–160 mm

Weight

80–165 grams

Appearance

The Philippine Tarsier is a small primate with fur ranging from gray to dark brown. It has exceptionally large, fixed eyes that provide excellent night vision, compensated by its ability to rotate its head 180°. Its elongated tarsal bones enable leaps of up to 3 meters, and its digits are tipped with rounded pads for gripping.

Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range Endemic to the southeastern Philippines
Habitats Primary and secondary tropical rainforests, scrub forests with dense vegetation
Specific Locations Bohol, Samar, Leyte, Mindanao, Maripipi, Siargao, Basilan, and Dinagat
Elevation Up to 750 meters (2,460 feet)

Philippine Tarsiers are found in the southeastern Philippines, inhabiting various forest types from primary rainforests to dense scrub vegetation at lower elevations.

Habits & Lifestyle

Diet Entirely carnivorous; feeds on insects, spiders, small crustaceans, and small vertebrates
Foraging Behavior Nocturnal hunters using keen eyesight and hearing; employs ambush tactics
Activity Pattern Nocturnal; sleeps during the day in dark hollows
Social Structure Generally solitary; may exhibit monogamous or polygamous mating patterns
Locomotion Arboreal; leaps between trees

Reproduction & Lifespan

Gestation Period Approximately 180 days (6 months)
Offspring Typically a single offspring
Newborn Development Born with fur and open eyes; capable of climbing shortly after birth
Lifespan Up to 24 years in the wild; shorter in captivity

Conservation Status

IUCN Red List Near Threatened (NT)
CITES Appendix II
Philippine Law Protected under Republic Act No. 9147

The Philippine Tarsier is listed as Near Threatened due to habitat loss and the threats posed by the illegal pet trade and stress from captivity.

Ecological Role

As nocturnal predators, Philippine Tarsiers play a role in controlling populations of insects and small vertebrates, contributing to the balance of their forest ecosystems. They are also considered indicator species for forest health.

Major Threats

The primary threats to Philippine Tarsiers include habitat destruction from various human activities, hunting and poaching for the pet trade and tourism, and the detrimental effects of stress from captivity.
