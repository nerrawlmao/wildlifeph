Gigantes Forest Frog

Platymantis insulatus

The Gigantes Forest Frog, also known as the Gigantes Limestone Frog, is a critically endangered amphibian species endemic to the Gigantes Islands in the Philippines, known for its slender body and unique habitat.

Common Name

Gigantes Forest Frog

Scientific Name

Platymantis insulatus

Snout-Vent Length (Male)

38–42 mm

Snout-Vent Length (Female)

40–45 mm

Appearance

The Gigantes Forest Frog has a relatively slender body with a narrow, rounded-pointed snout and a large tympanum. Its long, slender fingers lack webbing, while its toes have basal webbing, and the tips of both are dilated. The dorsal coloration varies from grayish olive green to brown, often with irregular dark blotches, and the hind limbs have narrow, dark cross bands.

Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range Endemic to the Gigantes Islands, Philippines
Specific Locations South Gigante Island and at least three other islands in the group
Habitats Limestone karst forests and caves in forested lowlands below 600 meters elevation
Cave Usage Moist and cool parts of caves for shelter; ventures into adjacent forests to feed

The Gigantes Forest Frog is found exclusively in the limestone karst forests and caves of the Gigantes Islands, utilizing caves for shelter and venturing into nearby forests to forage.

Habits & Lifestyle

Diet Presumed to be insectivorous, feeding on small invertebrates
Foraging Behavior Primarily nocturnal, foraging in forested areas adjacent to caves
Activity Pattern Nocturnal; spends daylight hours in caves
Social Structure Likely solitary outside of breeding periods

Reproduction & Lifespan

Development Direct development; no free-swimming tadpole stage
Egg Laying Eggs are laid on land
Fecundity Up to 48 eggs per individual
Lifespan Specific data lacking; potentially short lifespan in the wild

Conservation Status

IUCN Red List Critically Endangered (CR)
Philippine Law Listed as Critically Endangered under DENR Administrative Order No. 2019-09
Population Trend Considered to be in rapid decline

The Gigantes Forest Frog is critically endangered due to severe habitat loss and degradation, facing a rapid decline in its already limited population.

Ecological Role

As insectivores, Gigantes Forest Frogs help regulate insect populations within their unique limestone forest and cave ecosystems. Their sensitivity to environmental changes also makes them important indicators of habitat health.

Major Threats

The primary threats to the Gigantes Forest Frog include habitat loss and degradation from guano mining, limestone quarrying, deforestation, and increasing tourism. Pollution and human disturbance within their cave habitats further exacerbate their precarious situation.
