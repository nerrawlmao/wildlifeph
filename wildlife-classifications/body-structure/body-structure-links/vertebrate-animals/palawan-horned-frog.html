Palawan Horned Frog

Megophrys ligayae

The Palawan horned frog (Megophrys ligayae) is an endemic species to Palawan, Philippines. Known for its leaf-like appearance and horn-like projections above its eyes, this cryptic frog is well-camouflaged among the forest floor's leaf litter. It is a ground-dwelling amphibian found in moist tropical forests.

Appearance

The Palawan horned frog has a triangular-shaped head with pointed extensions above its eyes resembling horns. Its coloration mimics dead leaves, ranging from brown to tan with dark markings. This camouflage helps it avoid predators and ambush prey on the forest floor.

This frog inhabits the leaf litter of undisturbed forests, often near streams. It is terrestrial and uses its excellent camouflage to ambush insects and small invertebrates. It depends on a moist environment for survival and breeding.

Palawan horned frogs lay eggs in streams during the rainy season. The tadpoles develop in water and undergo metamorphosis before becoming terrestrial adults. Males produce mating calls at night to attract females.

Conservation Status

Population TrendDecreasing
IUCN Red ListVulnerable (VU)

The Palawan horned frog is considered Vulnerable due to deforestation and habitat degradation. Conservation efforts are needed to protect forest habitats in Palawan to prevent further decline.

Population Threats

Major threats include habitat loss due to logging, mining, and agricultural expansion. Pollution and climate change also impact the moist forest microhabitats essential for their survival and reproduction.

Population Number

The exact population is not documented, but trends indicate a decline. Protection of primary forest ecosystems in Palawan is critical for the species’ continued existence.

Ecological Niche

As insect predators, Palawan horned frogs help regulate insect populations. Their presence also indicates healthy forest floor ecosystems, making them important bioindicators of environmental quality.
