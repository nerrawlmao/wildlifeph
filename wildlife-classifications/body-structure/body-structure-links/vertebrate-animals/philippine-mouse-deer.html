Philippine Mouse-Deer

Tragulus nigricans

The Philippine Mouse-Deer is a small, nocturnal, and elusive ungulate endemic to the Philippines, specifically the islands of Balabac, Bugsuk, and Ramos. Despite its common name, it is not a true deer but belongs to the chevrotain family. Recognized for its role in local folklore and its ecological importance, this species is currently classified as Endangered due to habitat loss and hunting pressures.

Appearance

The Philippine Mouse-Deer has a sleek, dark brown coat with distinctive white stripes on its throat and chest. Its head features rufous or fulvous 'eyebrow' stripes extending from the eyes to the ears. Males lack antlers but possess elongated, tusk-like upper canines used for defense and territorial disputes. Its slender legs and arched back are adaptations for swift movement through dense underbrush.

Habitat & Ecology

    Biome Terrestrial Habitat Type Tropical lowland forests and mangrove areas Climate Zone Tropical

Endemic to the Philippines, the Philippine Mouse-Deer is primarily found on Balabac Island and nearby smaller islands such as Bugsuk and Ramos, located southwest of Palawan. They inhabit dense primary and secondary forests, often venturing into mangroves and open areas during nighttime foraging.

The Philippine Mouse-Deer exhibits a polygynous mating system. Females reach sexual maturity at around 5 months of age and can breed throughout the year. Offspring are precocial, being relatively mature and mobile shortly after birth, which aids in predator avoidance.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)

The species faces significant threats from habitat destruction due to agricultural expansion, particularly coconut plantations, and hunting for meat and the pet trade. Although protected under Philippine law, enforcement is often ineffective. Notably, the species does not occur in any of the country's 18 priority conservation sites.

Population Threats

Major threats to the Philippine Mouse-Deer include habitat loss from agricultural development, particularly the conversion of forests into coconut plantations, and hunting for meat, which is considered a delicacy. The species is also captured for the pet trade. Despite legal protections, enforcement is lacking, and the species is not present in any of the 18 priority conservation sites in the Philippines.

Population Number

Recent survey efforts estimate the total population size to be approximately 2,525 individuals, with a likely range between 1,635 and 3,461. However, exact numbers are difficult to ascertain due to the species' elusive nature and the challenges in conducting comprehensive field surveys.

Ecological Niche

As herbivores, Philippine Mouse-Deer play a vital role in their ecosystems by aiding in seed dispersal and contributing to forest regeneration. Their foraging habits help maintain the health and diversity of the forest undergrowth, making them important components of their native habitats.
