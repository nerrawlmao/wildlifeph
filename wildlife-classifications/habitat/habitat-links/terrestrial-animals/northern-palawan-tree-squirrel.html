Northern Palawan Tree Squirrel

Sundasciurus juvencus

The Northern Palawan tree squirrel (Sundasciurus juvencus) is a small rodent endemic to the forests of Palawan, Philippines. Agile and alert, this species plays an important role in seed dispersal and forest regeneration. It is arboreal, meaning it spends most of its life in trees.

Population Size

Unknown

Weight

140–200 g

Length

16–20 cm (body), 15–18 cm (tail)

Appearance

This squirrel has a short, dense fur coat that is typically gray-brown on the back and lighter underneath. It features a bushy tail almost as long as its body, used for balance and communication. It has sharp claws and a slender frame ideal for climbing trees.

Habitat & Ecology

    Biome Forest Habitat Type Lowland and montane tropical forests Climate Zone Tropical

This squirrel thrives in dense forests and is primarily arboreal. It feeds on fruits, seeds, and occasionally insects. Its activities help spread seeds and contribute to forest health. It prefers undisturbed habitats and is sensitive to habitat loss.

Mating Habits

Mating Season Year-round

Young Name Kit or Pup
Clutch Size 2–4 offspring
Incubation Period About 6 weeks

Northern Palawan tree squirrels reproduce year-round. The female raises the kits in tree nests, and the young are dependent on the mother for several weeks before becoming independent. Males may compete for mates but do not take part in rearing the young.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List Near Threatened (NT)

The Northern Palawan tree squirrel is considered Near Threatened due to ongoing habitat loss from logging and development. Although still found in parts of Palawan, its population is fragmented and in decline.

Population Threats

Habitat destruction from logging, agriculture, and urban development poses the biggest threat. As a forest-dependent species, the squirrel struggles to survive in fragmented or degraded habitats.

Population Number

Exact population numbers are unknown, but it is known to be in decline. Monitoring and protection of its native forest habitats are critical for its continued survival.

Ecological Niche

As seed dispersers and prey for forest predators, these squirrels are vital to the ecosystem. Their foraging activities support plant regeneration, contributing to overall forest biodiversity.
