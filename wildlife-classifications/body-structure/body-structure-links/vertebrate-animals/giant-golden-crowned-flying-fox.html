Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox

Acerodon jubatus

The giant golden-crowned flying fox (Acerodon jubatus), also known as the golden-capped fruit bat, is a species of megabat endemic to the Philippines. Since its description in 1831, three subspecies of the giant golden-crowned flying fox have been recognized, one of which is extinct

Appearance

the Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox is similar in appearance to many Pteropus species. It is different in its smaller canine teeth and its larger and more complex molars and premolars. Its upper incisors are slightly longer than Pteropus species, as well as sharper.

Habitat & Ecology

It is a forest specialist, occurring mostly at elevations from sea level to 1,100 m (3,600 ft). It prefers areas uninhabited by humans. A 2005 study found none in inhabited areas.

Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox mating habits are limited due to their elusive nature and the challenges of studying large, nocturnal, arboreal animals. Females breed approximately once every two years which is low reproduction rate, synchronized births with other females, the mating occurs year-round but the fertility is seasonal and they are presumed to be polygynous.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)

The population of the Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox (Acerodon jubatus) is decreasing at an alarming rate, leading to its classification as Endangered by the IUCN Red List.

Population Threats

The population is believed to be decreasing due to significant threats, primarily habitat loss from logging, agricultural conversion, and mining activities within its range in the Philippines. Also due to the fact that their reproduction is at low rate and misconception of them being a pest.

Population Number

The estimated population number for the Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox (Acerodon jubatus) is around 10,000 individuals, and probably no more than 20,000 mature individuals.

Ecological Niche

The ecological niche of the Giant Golden-crowned Flying Fox (Acerodon jubatus) is that of a large, highly mobile, nocturnal, frugivorous (and partly folivorous) mega-bat, serving as a critical keystone species for forest regeneration and health within the Philippines' dwindling lowland tropical rainforests. Serves as primary seed disperser and pollinator
