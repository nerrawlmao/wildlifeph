Philippine Crocodile

Crocodylus mindorensis

The Philippine crocodile (Crocodylus mindorensis) is a critically endangered freshwater crocodile species native to the Philippines. It inhabits rivers, lakes, marshes, and swamps, playing a crucial role in maintaining healthy aquatic ecosystems. Conservation efforts are ongoing to protect this rare species from extinction.

Population Size

Critically Low

Weight

Approx. 20–70 kg (varies by age)

Length

1.5–2.5 meters

Appearance

The Philippine crocodile is a medium-sized crocodilian with a broad snout and robust body. Its coloration ranges from brownish to dark gray, with some individuals showing lighter underbellies. It has powerful jaws with sharp teeth used for catching prey.

Philippine crocodiles live primarily in freshwater bodies with dense vegetation. They are apex predators, feeding on fish, amphibians, and small mammals. Their presence indicates a healthy aquatic ecosystem.

Conservation Status

Population TrendCritically Endangered
IUCN Red ListCritically Endangered (CR)

The Philippine crocodile is critically endangered due to habitat loss, hunting, and human conflict. Conservation programs focus on habitat protection, captive breeding, and reintroduction.

Population Threats

Major threats include habitat destruction from agriculture and development, hunting for skin and meat, and accidental capture in fishing gear. Its limited distribution makes recovery challenging.

Population Number

Fewer than 200 mature individuals remain in the wild, concentrated in isolated populations on Mindoro and nearby islands.

Ecological Niche

As an apex predator, the Philippine crocodile helps maintain balanced freshwater ecosystems by controlling prey populations and scavenging carrion, contributing to nutrient cycling.
