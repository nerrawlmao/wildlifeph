Visayan Spotted Deer

Rusa alfredi

The Visayan Spotted Deer, also known as the Philippine Spotted Deer or Prince Alfred's Deer, is an endangered deer species endemic to the Visayan islands of the Philippines, recognized by its distinct spotted coat.

Common Name

Visayan Spotted Deer

Scientific Name

Rusa alfredi

Body Length

Approximately 125–130 cm

Weight

25–80 kg

Appearance

The Visayan Spotted Deer has a dark brown coat adorned with distinctive beige or white spots that form an "A" pattern on its back and sides. It features cream-colored underparts and white fur on its chin and lower lip. Males possess short, thick, bumpy antlers.

Taxonomy

Kingdom Animalia
Phylum Chordata
Class Mammalia
Order Artiodactyla
Family Cervidae
Subfamily Cervinae
Genus Rusa

Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range Historically Visayan Islands; currently Panay and Negros
Habitats Primary and secondary forests, dense cogon grasslands, montane forests
Feeding Areas Burnt-out forest clearings

The Visayan Spotted Deer once ranged across several Visayan islands but is now primarily found in the forests and grasslands of Panay and Negros.

Habits & Lifestyle

Diet Herbivorous; feeds on grasses, leaves, buds, shoots, fruits, and flowers
Foraging Behavior Primarily nocturnal; forages at night, especially in clearings
Activity Pattern Nocturnal and shy
Social Structure Usually solitary or in small groups of up to three
Vocalization (Males) "Roaring" call similar to a dog's bark

Reproduction & Lifespan

Breeding Season No specific season; young seen year-round
Gestation Period Approximately 240 days (8 months)
Offspring Typically a single calf
Weaning At six months
Sexual Maturity Around one year
Lifespan Up to 18–19 years in captivity; likely shorter in the wild

Conservation Status

IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)
Philippine Law Protected under Republic Act No. 9147
Population Estimate Fewer than 300 individuals remain in the wild

The Visayan Spotted Deer is endangered, with a critically low wild population primarily found on Panay and Negros islands, facing threats from habitat loss and hunting.

Ecological Role

As herbivores, Visayan Spotted Deer influence vegetation dynamics by feeding on various plant species, potentially shaping plant community compositions, especially in disturbed areas.

Major Threats

The primary threats to the Visayan Spotted Deer are habitat loss due to deforestation, logging, and agricultural expansion, as well as hunting for meat and the pet trade, and the fragmentation of their populations.
