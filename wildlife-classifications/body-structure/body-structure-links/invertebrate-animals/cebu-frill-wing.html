Cebu Frill-wing

Risiocnemis seidenschwarzi

The Cebu Frill-wing is a beautiful, diminutive, and highly specialized insect, whose very existence underscores the urgent need for forest and freshwater conservation on Cebu Island.

Appearance

Like all damselflies, it has a very slender, elongated body compared to its close relatives, the dragonflies. Its wings are held together and usually over its body when at rest, unlike dragonflies which hold their wings spread out.

The Cebu Frill-wing (Risiocnemis seidenschwarzi) has an incredibly specific and fragile habitat and ecological niche, which is the primary reason for its critically endangered status. The Cebu Frill-wing occupies a niche as a highly specialized, stenotopic (tolerating only a narrow range of environmental conditions) predator within a very specific, diminishing forest-stream ecosystem.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List ritically Endangered (CR)

It is listed as Critically Endangered by the IUCN Red List and was even highlighted in 2007 as one of the "World's 100 Most Threatened Species" by the Zoological Society of London. Its extremely restricted range and highly specialized habitat make it exceptionally vulnerable to deforestation and pollution.

Population Threats

The Cebu Frill-wing is on the brink of extinction because its specific and fragile habitat, the lowland forests and pristine streams of Cebu, have been almost entirely destroyed by human activities.

Population Number

The precise population number for the Cebu Frill-wing (Risiocnemis seidenschwarzi) is unknown, and it's considered extremely small and severely fragmented.

Ecological Niche

The Cebu Frill-wing occupies a very precise and fragile niche as a sentinel species for Cebu's remaining natural heritage. Its ecological importance lies not in being a major food source or a widespread predator, but in its absolute dependence on, and therefore its ability to signal the health of, one of the most critically threatened and biodiverse ecosystems in the Philippines. Its precarious existence highlights the devastating impact of deforestation and pollution on unique species.
