Tamaraw

Bubalus mindorensis

The Tamaraw, also known as the Mindoro dwarf buffalo, is a rare and critically endangered bovine species endemic to the island of Mindoro in the Philippines.

Common Name

Tamaraw

Scientific Name

Bubalus mindorensis

Shoulder Height

95–120 cm

Weight

180–300 kg

Appearance

The Tamaraw has a stocky, heavyset body with a dark brown to grayish coloration. Both males and females possess short, V-shaped horns. Distinctive white markings are present on their hooves and the inner lower forelegs. They also have more hair compared to the common water buffalo.

Taxonomy

Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range Endemic to the island of Mindoro, Philippines
Current Range Restricted to a few remote areas, primarily within Mounts Iglit-Baco National Park and Aruyan
Preferred Habitats Tropical highland forests, thick brush near open glades, grassy plains, areas close to water sources

The Tamaraw is exclusively found on the island of Mindoro in the Philippines, inhabiting diverse environments ranging from highland forests to grassy plains.

Habits & Lifestyle

Diet Primarily herbivorous, feeding on grasses, young bamboo shoots, and wild sugarcane
Foraging Behavior Naturally diurnal, with some adapting to nocturnal feeding to avoid human activity
Social Structure Generally solitary and reclusive; not typically found in herds
Temperament Known to be aggressive when threatened

Reproduction & Lifespan

Gestation Period Approximately 300 days
Offspring Typically one calf every two years
Calf Independence Calves remain with the mother for 2–4 years
Lifespan Estimated between 20–25 years in the wild

Conservation Status

IUCN Red List Critically Endangered (CR)
CITES Appendix I
Philippine Law Protected under Republic Act No. 9147
Population Estimate Approximately 30 to 200 individuals remaining in the wild

The Tamaraw is critically endangered, with a very small population remaining in the wild, facing severe threats to its survival.

Ecological Role

As grazers, Tamaraws play a crucial role in maintaining the balance of grassland and forest ecosystems by controlling vegetation growth.

Major Threats

The Tamaraw faces significant threats including habitat loss due to logging, agriculture, and infrastructure development. Hunting and poaching, diseases from domestic livestock, and competition for grazing land further endanger the species.
