Whale Shark Philippines

Rhincodon typus

The Whale Shark, locally known as Butanding or Balilan in the Philippines, is the world's largest fish, a gentle giant filter-feeder found in tropical and warm temperate seas.

Common Name

Whale Shark

Scientific Name

Rhincodon typus

Average Length

10–12 meters

Weight

Up to 21.5 metric tons

Appearance

The Whale Shark has a broad, flattened head with a large terminal mouth. Its dark gray to bluish body is covered in a distinctive pattern of light spots and stripes. The skin is thick and rough, and it has three prominent ridges along each side of its body.

Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range Circumglobal in tropical and warm temperate seas
Philippine Presence Donsol (Sorsogon), Oslob (Cebu), Tubbataha Reefs
Habitats Coastal and open waters, near coral reefs, areas with high plankton concentrations

Whale Sharks are found globally in tropical and warm temperate seas. In the Philippines, they are frequently sighted in areas like Donsol, Oslob, and Tubbataha Reefs, often in coastal and open waters rich in plankton.

Diet & Foraging

Diet Filter-feeds on plankton, copepods, krill, fish eggs, small nektonic organisms
Foraging Behavior Employs ram filtration and active suction feeding; often near the surface

Reproduction & Lifespan

Reproduction Ovoviviparous; females carry hundreds of embryos
Lifespan Estimated up to 80–130 years
Sexual Maturity (Males) Around 25 years of age

Conservation Status

IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)
CITES Appendix II
CMS Appendix I

The Whale Shark is listed as Endangered globally, facing threats from fishing, bycatch, vessel strikes, unsustainable tourism, and habitat degradation.

Ecological Role

As filter feeders, Whale Sharks help regulate plankton populations, contributing to marine ecosystem balance. Their migrations also aid in nutrient distribution across oceanic regions.

Major Threats

Major threats to Whale Sharks include targeted hunting and bycatch in fisheries, vessel strikes due to their surface feeding behavior, unsustainable tourism practices, climate change affecting their prey, and habitat degradation from coastal development and pollution.
