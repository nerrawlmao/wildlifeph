Philippine Long-Tailed Macaque

Macaca fascicularis philippensis

The Philippine Long-Tailed Macaque is a subspecies of the crab-eating macaque, endemic to the Philippines. Known locally as "unggoy" or "matsing," these primates are highly adaptable, inhabiting various forested environments across the archipelago. They play a crucial role in their ecosystems, particularly in seed dispersal.

Appearance

Philippine Long-Tailed Macaques have a reddish-brown to grayish-brown coat, with lighter underparts. Males are generally larger and may have darker fur compared to females. They possess expressive faces, long downward-curved tails, and cheek pouches for storing food.

Habitat & Ecology

    Biome Terrestrial Habitat Type Tropical and subtropical forests, including mangroves and secondary forests Climate Zone Tropical

This subspecies is found throughout the Philippines, including islands such as Luzon, Mindanao, Palawan, and the Visayas. They inhabit various forest types, from lowland rainforests to montane and mangrove forests, and are often found near human settlements.

Philippine Long-Tailed Macaques have a polygynous mating system. Females give birth to a single infant, which is cared for primarily by the mother, though other group members may also assist.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)

The species faces threats from habitat destruction, hunting, and capture for the pet trade and biomedical research. Conservation efforts are needed to address these challenges and protect remaining populations.

Population Threats

Major threats include deforestation for agriculture and urban development, hunting for food and the pet trade, and capture for use in biomedical research. Human-wildlife conflict also poses significant risks, as macaques may raid crops or become aggressive in areas where they have become habituated to human presence.

Population Number

While exact population numbers are not well-documented, the overall trend is a decline due to the aforementioned threats. Conservation measures are essential to prevent further population decreases.

Ecological Niche

As omnivores and frugivores, Philippine Long-Tailed Macaques play a vital role in seed dispersal, contributing to forest regeneration. Their foraging behavior also helps control insect populations, maintaining ecological balance within their habitats.
