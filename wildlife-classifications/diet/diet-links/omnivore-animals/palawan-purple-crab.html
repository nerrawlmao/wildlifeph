Palawan Purple Crab

Insulamon palawanense

The Palawan purple crab (Insulamon palawanense) is a striking freshwater crab endemic to the island of Palawan in the Philippines. Known for its vivid purple coloration, this species was only discovered and described in 2012. It lives in freshwater streams and is highly dependent on clean, unpolluted habitats.

Appearance

This crab is renowned for its deep purple to violet coloration, which distinguishes it from other crab species. It has a flat, rounded carapace and strong claws that it uses for feeding and defense. Its vivid color may serve as camouflage among river rocks and foliage.

Habitat & Ecology

The Palawan purple crab inhabits clean, freshwater streams and rivers in forested areas of Palawan. It is mostly active at night and hides under rocks and leaf litter during the day. The crab plays a role in nutrient cycling within its ecosystem.

Palawan purple crabs lay eggs in moist environments, and females carry the fertilized eggs until they hatch. The young undergo direct development, emerging as miniature versions of adults without a larval stage.

Conservation Status

Population TrendDecreasing
IUCN Red ListVulnerable (VU)

This species is considered Vulnerable due to its restricted range and sensitivity to water pollution, habitat loss, and deforestation. Conservation efforts are vital to preserve freshwater systems in Palawan.

Population Threats

Primary threats include habitat destruction due to mining, agriculture, and deforestation, as well as water pollution. Its limited range and dependence on clean freshwater make it especially vulnerable.

Population Number

Exact population numbers are unknown. However, surveys suggest declining numbers, highlighting the importance of monitoring and freshwater conservation in Palawan.

Ecological Niche

As both predator and scavenger, the Palawan purple crab helps control insect populations and recycles nutrients in freshwater ecosystems, supporting the health of tropical stream habitats.
