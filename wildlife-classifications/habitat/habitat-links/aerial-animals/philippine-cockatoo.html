Philippine Cockatoo

Cacatua haematuropygia

The Philippine cockatoo (Cacatua haematuropygia) is a medium-sized, white cockatoo native to the Philippines. It is known for its striking red tail feathers and is classified as critically endangered due to habitat loss and trapping.

Population Size

Critically Endangered

Weight

300–450 g

Length

35–38 cm

Appearance

The Philippine cockatoo is predominantly white with a prominent red patch under its tail. It has a crest of feathers on top of its head that it can raise or lower. Its robust beak is adapted for cracking nuts and seeds.

Habitat & Ecology

    BiomeTropical forest Habitat TypeLowland forests, mangroves, coastal areas Climate ZoneTropical

This cockatoo inhabits lowland forests and mangrove areas in the western Philippines. It feeds on seeds, fruits, nuts, and occasionally insects. It nests in tree cavities.

Philippine cockatoos nest in tree cavities. The female incubates the eggs while the male feeds her. The chicks fledge after about 10 weeks.

Conservation Status

Population TrendDecreasing
IUCN Red ListCritically Endangered (CR)

The Philippine cockatoo faces severe threats from habitat destruction and illegal wildlife trade. Conservation efforts include habitat protection and captive breeding programs.

Population Threats

Primary threats include deforestation, habitat fragmentation, and trapping for the pet trade.

Population Number

Current population estimates suggest fewer than 1,000 mature individuals remain in the wild.

Ecological Niche

The Philippine cockatoo plays an important role in seed dispersal in its tropical forest habitat, helping maintain forest diversity.
