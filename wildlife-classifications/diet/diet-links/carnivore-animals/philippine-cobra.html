Philippine Cobra

Naja philippinensis

The Philippine cobra (Naja philippinensis) is a highly venomous spitting cobra endemic to the northern Philippines. Known for its potent neurotoxic venom and ability to spit venom up to 3 meters, it inhabits lowland plains, forests, agricultural fields, and areas near water bodies. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Appearance

The Philippine cobra is a stocky, medium-length snake with a hood formed by elongated cervical ribs. Adults are uniformly light to medium brown, while juveniles are darker. The head is slightly distinct from the neck with large nostrils and round pupils. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Conservation Status

Population TrendUnknown
IUCN Red ListNot Evaluated

While not currently evaluated by the IUCN, the Philippine cobra faces threats from habitat destruction and human persecution. Its presence near human settlements often leads to conflict.

Population Threats

Major threats include habitat loss due to deforestation and agricultural expansion, as well as killing by humans out of fear. The species' proximity to human habitation increases the likelihood of such encounters.

Population Number

Exact population numbers are unknown. Further research is needed to assess the species' conservation status and population trends.

Ecological Niche

As a predator of rodents and other small animals, the Philippine cobra plays a role in controlling these populations, thus maintaining ecological balance in its habitat.
