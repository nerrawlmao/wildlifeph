Luzon Peacock Swallowtail

Papilio chikae

The Luzon peacock swallowtail (Papilio chikae) is a stunning butterfly species endemic to Luzon, Philippines. It is famous for its brilliant metallic blue and green iridescent wings and distinctive peacock-like markings. This rare butterfly is highly prized by collectors and is currently endangered due to habitat destruction and illegal wildlife trade.

Appearance

The male Luzon peacock swallowtail displays shimmering green scales and vivid blue patches on its hindwings, resembling the eye spots of a peacock. Females are slightly larger and less vibrant. Both sexes have long tails extending from their hindwings, characteristic of swallowtail butterflies.

Habitat & Ecology

    Biome Forest Habitat Type Montane and tropical forests Climate Zone Tropical

The Luzon peacock swallowtail inhabits high-altitude tropical forests in northern Luzon. Its larvae feed on local citrus and related plants. Adults are active during daylight, feeding on nectar and playing a role in pollination. This butterfly prefers dense, undisturbed forest areas for survival.

The Luzon peacock swallowtail lays its eggs on host plants. After hatching, larvae feed on the leaves before forming chrysalises and emerging as adults. The mating process includes a courtship display involving flight patterns and pheromone signals. This species typically breeds once or twice a year depending on environmental conditions.

Conservation Status

Population Trend Decreasing
IUCN Red List Endangered (EN)

The Luzon peacock swallowtail is listed as Endangered due to its small population size, limited distribution, and threats from habitat destruction and illegal collection. It is protected under international and Philippine conservation laws.

Population Threats

Major threats to this butterfly include deforestation, habitat fragmentation, and illegal poaching for the butterfly trade. Forest clearing for agriculture and development has reduced suitable habitats, placing the species at high risk.

Population Number

Exact numbers for the Luzon peacock swallowtail are unknown, but the population is considered very small and fragmented. It is listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List due to declining trends and limited range.

Ecological Niche

As pollinators and prey, Luzon peacock swallowtails contribute to forest ecosystem health and food chains. Their larvae feed on native plants, and adults assist in plant reproduction by feeding on nectar.
